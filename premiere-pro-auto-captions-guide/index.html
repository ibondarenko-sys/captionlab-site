What Adobe's auto-captions actually do

Premiere Pro's Speech to Text (Window → Text → Transcript) generates a transcript from your sequence's audio, which you can then convert into a caption track with Create Captions.

✅

What it's good at

Fast, free with Creative Cloud, and reasonably accurate for its supported languages — mainly English and a handful of others.

⚠️

Where it falls short

No offline mode, and roughly 80+ languages (Ukrainian, Polish, Turkish, Arabic, and many more) aren't supported at all.

🧩

How to fill the gap

A plugin like CaptionLab adds the missing languages and an offline option, using the same kind of Premiere Pro caption-track output.

Choosing the right approach for your project

For English-language Creative Cloud subscribers, Adobe's built-in tool is a perfectly good default. For any of the ~83 languages Adobe doesn't cover, for footage that can't be processed in the cloud, or for editors who'd rather not depend on an active Creative Cloud subscription just for captions, CaptionLab covers the gap with a one-time $39 purchase.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use Adobe's captions and CaptionLab in the same project?
Yes — nothing stops you from using Adobe's tool for English sequences and CaptionLab for sequences in other languages, in the same Premiere Pro project.
Does Adobe's auto-caption feature cost extra?
It's included with a Creative Cloud subscription that includes Premiere Pro — no separate fee, but no offline option either.
How accurate are Premiere Pro's auto-captions generally?
Reasonably accurate for clear audio in a supported language; like any automatic transcription, accuracy drops with background noise, heavy accents, or overlapping speech.
